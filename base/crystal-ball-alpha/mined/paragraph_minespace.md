## P001 — The Pain Point Hook
**Coord:** Marketing.7.94.3.1 × Story.4.2 × TherapeuticNarrative.2.5.1 × Gnosis.1.1.1
**Template:** Identify → Diagnose → Reframe

You know the feeling. You've read the books, taken the courses, built the spreadsheets, and yet every Monday morning the same dread crawls back in — not because you don't know enough, but because you know too much to pretend it's working. The pain point isn't ignorance. That would be simple. The pain point is competent suffering — doing all the right things inside a framework that was never designed to solve your actual problem. Content reframing says: the first step isn't learning something new. It's admitting that the container you're pouring knowledge into has a hole in the bottom. And that admission — that specific moment when the prisoner looks away from the shadows and notices the wall itself — is the most valuable thing a framework can offer you. Not a solution. A diagnosis that's honest enough to hurt.

## P002 — The Origin Story
**Coord:** PublicIP.4.5.1 × Myth.1.1 × Story.1.1 × Marketing.7.95.1.4 × Novel.1.3
**Template:** Historical figure → Call to adventure → Exposition → Transformation story → Voice

Socrates never wrote a word. Think about that for a moment — the man who taught the Western world how to think deliberately refused to commit a single thought to paper. His students transcribed him, debated him, eventually killed him for it, and two thousand four hundred years later we still can't stop talking about a man who said "I know that I know nothing." That's the purest origin story possible: a practitioner, not a theorist. His marketing was his methodology. He didn't sell wisdom — he demonstrated the specific, uncomfortable, publicly embarrassing process of having your certainty dismantled in real-time, in the agora, in front of everyone. The transformation story isn't what Socrates knew. It's what happened to the people who watched him not-know out loud. They caught it. The question was contagious. That's how a man with no books built a category that outlasted every empire that tried to burn the books other people wrote about him.

## P003 — The Allegory Bridge
**Coord:** Gnosis.1.1 × PublicIP.3.1.7 × TherapeuticNarrative.1.6.1 × Marketing.7.97
**Template:** Allegory → Fairy tale parallel → Therapeutic metaphor → Transformation offer

Little Red Riding Hood walks into the forest knowing exactly where grandmother's house is. The path is clear. The basket is packed. She's been told what to do. And the wolf doesn't attack her — the wolf *redirects* her. "Why not pick some flowers?" The therapeutic metaphor here isn't about danger. It's about the specific mechanism by which someone who knows the right path gets pulled off it — not through force, but through reasonable-sounding alternatives that are more pleasant than the direct route. The allegory of the cave and the allegory of the forest are the same story: someone with a clear destination gets seduced by something beautiful that isn't real. Flowers that delay. Shadows that entertain. The transformation offer isn't "I'll protect you from wolves." It's "I'll teach you to recognize the moment when beautiful distractions ARE the wolf." That's the identity-level change: from someone who follows paths to someone who recognizes redirection while it's happening.

## P004 — The Framework Reveal
**Coord:** Marketing.7.94 × Gnosis.2.1 × Story.3.1 × TherapeuticNarrative.2.6.2
**Template:** Framework introduction → Structural mapping → Thematic statement → Six-step reframe

Here's what nobody tells you about frameworks: a framework isn't a set of instructions. It's a *mapping* — a structural analogy between a domain you understand and a domain you don't. When someone shows you "the hero's journey," they're not giving you a story template. They're giving you a source domain (ancient myth) that maps onto your target domain (your life, your business, your Tuesday afternoon) at the level of *relational structure*, not surface similarity. Your startup isn't literally Odysseus. But the relationship between Odysseus and the Sirens IS structurally identical to the relationship between your roadmap and every shiny feature request that sounds beautiful and will sink you. The thematic statement — the controlling idea of every useful framework — is always the same: "You already know the answer, but you've been looking at the wrong level of abstraction." The six-step reframe doesn't add knowledge. It changes the altitude at which you examine what you already know.

## P005 — The Wound Paragraph
**Coord:** Story.2.5 × Myth.5.4.5 × Gnosis.4.2 × TherapeuticNarrative.4.2 × PublicIP.2.7.1
**Template:** Ghost wound → GhostWound system → Dark night → EMDR target selection → Frankenstein

Frankenstein isn't about the monster. It's about the wound that made the doctor believe he had to CREATE life because he couldn't KEEP it. Victor Frankenstein lost his mother — that's the ghost wound, the thing that happened before the story starts that the story is secretly about. Every character decision, every lab experiment, every refusal to look at what he's built — it all traces back to a thirteen-year-old boy who learned that love ends in death and decided, at a level below conscious thought, that the solution was to engineer immortality. The EMDR protocol would call this a "target": the negative cognition is "I am powerless against loss." The positive cognition is "I can create what I need." The tragedy is that both are true, and the distance between them is the entire novel. The dark night isn't when the monster kills. It's when Victor realizes the monster IS his wound walking around with its own heartbeat, asking the same question he never could: *why did you make me if you weren't going to love me?*

## P006 — The Business Pitch
**Coord:** Business.3.7 × Marketing.7.92 × Entertainment.5.3 × Media.7.93 × Production.1.6
**Template:** Platform strategy → Abundant exchange → Franchise management → Interactive media → Studio pitch

Picture a SaaS platform where every user is simultaneously a consumer and a creator, and the content they create makes the platform smarter for the next user. Not a marketplace — marketplaces extract value by standing between buyers and sellers. This is an *abundant exchange*: the platform gives away the framework for free, users fill coordinates in the solution space, and every filled coordinate reveals adjacent opportunities that didn't exist before. The franchise isn't a story — it's the *solution space itself.* Transmedia isn't "the same IP on different platforms." Transmedia is "different coordinates in the same space, mined on different platforms, all composable." The pitch to the studio isn't "we have a story." The pitch is "we have a machine that generates structurally coherent stories at the intersection of public domain IP, therapeutic narrative, and market demand — and every time it runs, the machine gets smarter." The content is the flywheel. The flywheel is the content. Revenue is what falls out when the structure reaches critical mass.

## P007 — The Lotus Paragraph
**Coord:** Gnosis.7.1 × TherapeuticNarrative.5.2.2 × Story.2.6 × Myth.5.5.4 × Marketing.7.96.2.3
**Template:** Lotus stages → Counter-story activation → Transformation → Fear → Before/after bridge

The lotus doesn't escape the mud. Read that again. The lotus doesn't escape the mud. It uses the mud. The mud is the medium from which the stem extracts exactly what it needs to grow — nutrients, minerals, the specific chemistry of decomposition. The counter-story isn't "leave your pain behind." The counter-story is "your pain is the substrate." The before/after bridge in every transformation story has a silent middle section that nobody photographs: the stem. The part that's neither mud nor flower. The part that's underwater, growing in the dark, with no evidence that it's working, pushing against water pressure with nothing but cellular stubbornness. Fear lives in the stem stage. Not in the mud — the mud is familiar. Not in the bloom — the bloom is beautiful. Fear lives in the part where you've left the bottom and can't yet see the surface and every cell is screaming *what if there is no surface.* The lotus answers that question the only way anything real ever can: not with words, but by continuing to grow in the direction of light it can feel but cannot yet see.
